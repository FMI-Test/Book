## Deciphering the Name and Titles

![Mirza Agha Khan Mumtahen-ul-Mulk — Deciphering the Name and Titles](images/Kargozar-Titles.webp)
*Mirza Agha Khan Mumtahen-ul-Mulk Aliyar Fakhran — Deciphering the Name and Titles*

The full name and title — **Mirza Agha Khan Mumtahen-ul-Mulk Aliyar Fakhran** — is a layered artifact of Qajar-era Persian nomenclature. Each element carried specific meaning in 19th- and early 20th-century Iran.

### Mirza (میرزا)

The meaning of *Mirza* depended entirely on its placement:

- **Before the name** (e.g., *Mirza Hassan*): designated a **literate man** — a clerk, secretary, or educated professional. In an era when literacy was rare, this marked membership in the administrative class.
- **After the name** (e.g., *Abbas Mirza*): indicated **royal or princely status**, reserved for sons of the Shah or high-ranking Qajar nobles.

By the early 1900s, "Mirza" was the standard title for accountants, scribes (*Mirza-ye Benevise*), and government bureaucrats staffing the newly formed ministries. In **1935**, Reza Shah Pahlavi abolished all aristocratic titles — *Mirza*, *Khan*, *Agha* — as part of sweeping modernization reforms. "Agha" (Mr.) became the universal address for all Iranian men.

Today, "Mirza" survives primarily in surnames: *Mirzakhani*, *Mirzazadeh*, *Amir-Mirza*. The renowned mathematician **Maryam Mirzakhani** (1977–2017), Fields Medal winner, carried the legacy of this intellectual class in her family name.

| Period | Primary Meaning | Social Context |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1900–1935** | Literate professional or Prince | Highly respected; indicated status or education |
| **Post-1935** | Legally abolished as a title | Replaced by "Agha" for formal address |
| **Late 1900s** | Part of a surname or archaic honorific | Linked to family history rather than profession |

In 20th-century Iran, spanning the end of the Qajar dynasty and the entirety of the Pahlavi era, the term **Aqa** (آقا) served as a versatile title of respect, though its specific connotations shifted depending on social class and context.

### Mr., Sir, or Master

At its most basic level, [آقا (Aqa)](https://www.google.com/search?q=https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%25D8%25A2%25D9%2582%25D8%25A4) was used as the standard prefix for "Mr." or "Sir." However, during these eras, it carried deeper weight:

  * **Social Status:** In the Qajar period, it was often used to address landowners, merchants, or men of notable standing.
  * **Family Dynamics:** It was (and sometimes still is) a respectful way for children to refer to their father or for a wife to refer to her husband in formal or traditional households.
  * **The "Master" Connotation:** Derived from Turkic roots, it historically meant "elder brother" or "lord," implying a level of authority or mastery over a household or business.

### Religious and Political Usage

In the 20th century, "Aqa" took on specific significance within religious and clerical circles:

  * **Clerical Respect:** It was a common honorific for high-ranking clerics and Ayatollahs.
  * **The Supreme Leader:** In the latter part of the century (post-1979), "Aqa" became a common shorthand used by followers to refer specifically to the Supreme Leader, denoting a unique level of singular authority.

### Khan (خان)

During the **Qajar era** (1789–1925), *Khan* was an extremely common title for **noblemen, courtiers, and tribal leaders**:

- **Tribal Authority:** The standard title for heads of powerful nomadic tribes (Bakhtiari, Qashqai). These Khans held significant autonomous local power.
- **Bureaucratic Rank:** Bestowed by the Shah upon high-ranking officials and military commanders.
- **Polite Address:** By the 19th century, it was increasingly used as a general honorific attached after a man's name (e.g., *Dariush Khan*), carrying more formal weight than *Agha*.

**Etymological roots:**

- **Turko-Mongol:** From Old Turkic *qaɣan* ("ruler/king") — the source of the political title.
- **Middle Persian:** From *xān* ("house/inn"), which gave us *Caravanserai* (roadside inn for travelers).

### Mumtahen-ul-Mulk (ممتحن‌الملک)

A high-ranking aristocratic title meaning **"The Proven of the Realm"** or **"The Tested of the Kingdom."**

| Component | Meaning | Significance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Mumtahen** (ممتحن) | Tested / Examined / Proven | Someone who has passed rigorous trials |
| **Al** (ال) | The | Arabic definite article |
| **Mulk** (ملک) | Kingdom / Realm | Represents the Persian state |

Titles ending in *-ul-Mulk* were granted by the Shah to elite civil servants, diplomats, and intellectuals. *Mumtahen-ul-Mulk* specifically implied a person of **proven merit** who had been "tested" through rigorous education or service. After the 1935 surname reforms, many holders adopted their title as a permanent family name (*Momtahen*, *Momtahen-ol-Molk*).

### Aliyar (علی‌یار)

A name of Turkic-Persian origin meaning **"Friend of Ali"** or **"Helper of Ali"**, common among the traditional elite and warrior classes of Northeastern Iran (Khorasan).

### Fakhran (فخران)

Derived from *Fakhr* (فخر), meaning **"Pride"** or **"Honor."** Adopted as a permanent family surname during the 1935 reforms, marking a lineage of established noble standing. The name signifies "The Proud Ones" or "The Honored Ones."

## Historical Context: The Landlord-Peasant System and Reza Shah's Reforms

Before the 1935 reforms, Iranian society was rigidly stratified under the **landlord-peasant system** (*Nezam-e Arbab-va Ra'iyat*):

- **Khans and Aghas** held exclusive titles denoting landed nobility and administrative authority.
- **Peasants and laborers** had no formal titles and existed at the bottom of the feudal order.
- **Women** were addressed only as *Khanoom* (upper-class) or had no formal address at all.

Reza Shah's 1935 decree was a radical break: all men became *Agha* (Mr.), all women became *Banoo* or *Khanoom* (Mrs./Lady), regardless of birth or wealth. Aristocratic titles (*Khan*, *Mirza*, *-ul-Mulk*) were abolished to forge a **uniform, egalitarian nation**.

The reform's promise, however, diverged sharply from practice. Economic inequality persisted. The landlord-peasant structure remained largely intact. Mohammad Reza Shah's later reforms — land redistribution, industrialization, currency stabilization — accelerated development but deepened internal contradictions. By 1979, these erupted in the Iranian Revolution, overthrowing a monarchy tracing its lineage 2,500 years through civilizations spanning 7,000 years. The irony: Reza Shah's egalitarian vision of "all Iranians as equals" could not survive the very modernization that birthed it.

## Biography of Aliyar Fakhran

**Aliyar Fakhran**, formally **Mirza Agha Khan Mumtahen-ul-Mulk**, was born in **1285 AH (1868 AD)** in Jajarm — a district of Bojnourd in Khorasan province, now a city in its own right. He was educated at **Dar al-Fonun** in Tehran, Iran's first modern institution of higher learning, and at the **Muslim Higher School in Tbilisi** (then part of the Russian Empire), which gave him the linguistic and cultural fluency necessary for his future career.

He had one daughter and four sons. His eldest son, **Bagher Khan Fakhran**, studied at Dar al-Fonun and died at the age of twenty-seven. **Malikeh Fakhran** was Bagher Khan's daughter.

![Dar al-Fonun](images/Ali-Yar-Fakhran-Dar-Al-Fonun.webp)

### The "Bridge" Statesman

During the transition from the Qajar dynasty to Reza Shah, Aliyar Fakhran occupied a rare position: a survivor of the old bureaucracy competent enough to lead the new one. His career bridged old-world Persian diplomacy and the centralized Pahlavi administration.

| Role | Impact |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Governor (Bojnourd & Quchan)** | Represented central government authority in sensitive Khorasan border regions during the shift from tribal rule to a centralized state |
| **Head of Justice (Bojnourd)** | Helped transition Iran from religious *Sharia* courts to a secular, codified judicial system |
| **Diplomat (Tbilisi, St. Petersburg, Batumi)** | Iran's eyes and ears in the Russian Empire and later the Soviet Union, during Russia's most violent transitions |
| **Ambassador Plenipotentiary (Leningrad)** | One of the elite diplomats trusted by the Pahlavi regime to navigate the complex relationship with the USSR |

His nickname **"Kargozar"** (کارگزار) — meaning **Governor/Agent** — refers to the specialized branch of the Foreign Ministry that handled legal, commercial, and administrative affairs. As a *Kargozar*, he was not just a political representative; he acted as a high-level magistrate responsible for the legal protection of thousands of Iranian merchants and citizens abroad.

> **Translation Note:** *Kargozar* is frequently mistranslated as "clerk," "waiter," or "broker." In Qajar-era administrative terminology, it specifically denoted a **Governor** or **Agent** — a senior official with broad judicial and executive powers representing the central government. The closest modern equivalent would be "Governor" or "Regional Agent of the State."

## The Leningrad Mission

His final and highest diplomatic posting was as **Ambassador Plenipotentiary** (*Sefir Kabir*) of Iran to **Leningrad** (formerly St. Petersburg). This was one of the most sensitive posts in the world during the early 20th century.

### The Diplomatic Environment

- **Navigating the Bolshevik Rise:** He served during the collapse of the Russian Empire and the emergence of the Soviet Union, maintaining Iranian interests while the host government was being completely restructured.
- **The 1921 Treaty of Friendship:** A landmark event for any Iranian diplomat in Russia. The treaty cancelled Tsarist debts and concessions but introduced Article 6, giving the Soviets a pretext to intervene on Iranian soil — a point of contention for decades.
- **Protection of Iranians:** As a trained *Kargozar*, he was responsible for the legal protection of thousands of Iranian merchants and workers in the Caucasus and Russia.
- **Multilingualism:** To serve as Ambassador in Leningrad, he was fluent in **Russian**, **French** (the language of international diplomacy), and likely **Turkic dialects** from his years in Tbilisi and Batumi.

### Connection to Dar al-Fonun

His education at [Dar al-Fonun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dar_al-Fonun) was significant. This school was the breeding ground for the "New Men" of Iran — intellectuals who moved from purely religious education toward secular statecraft. He was part of the first generation of scientifically and linguistically trained bureaucrats, which explains his *Mumtahen-ul-Mulk* ("Proven of the Realm") title.

## Garmeh, Jajarm, Ivar, Darq

![Garmeh, Jajarm, Ivar, Darqe](images/Garmeh-Jajarm-Ivar-Darq.webp)
*AI illustration: Garmeh, Jajarm, Ivar, Darq*

Garmeh and Jajarm are two towns approximately 10 kilometers apart, situated along the road that cuts through the Golestan Forest from Gorgan toward Bojnourd. Fifty years ago, several kilometers past the Golestan Forest on the right side of the Gorgan-Bojnourd-Mashhad road, there stood a ruined historical fortress — an Achaemenid postal relay station (*Chapar-khaneh*) with a history stretching back 2,500 years, and a gas station shortly after.

The road was stunningly beautiful — sharp hairpin turns like the famous Chalus Road, winding up and down through dense forest. But the death toll was horrific: blind curves on the beautiful forest road, combined with the impatience of inexperienced drivers who took the bends at reckless speed, crashing head-on into oncoming traffic or wrapping themselves around trees. Countless bloody tragedies — including one that nearly claimed the author himself, not in the Golestan Forest, but a few kilometers outside Mashhad.

### A Road in Wonderland

Once a dirt track, then a gravel highway, then two-lane asphalt, and presumably today a multi-lane motorway. In the old days, it was a desert truck route through hills and mountains — deep ruts carved by rain, branching paths where one or two forks led nowhere and the rest led right. Cars navigated the ridges between ruts with extraordinary skill, choosing correct paths by instinct alone, until the pass ended and it was all desert — wide open roads to the outskirts of Garmeh.

The main street of Garmeh led to the town square, and from there a beautiful route to Jajarm. Like Amirkola clinging to Babol, there was an ancient defunct mill, ice-cold spring water, green trees whose leaves danced in the fiery desert breeze under the sun. A rest stop and picnic ground for weekends and weary travelers.

At the entrance to Jajarm stood the gendarmerie outpost — a relic of the Reza Shah era with its French name and the discipline of Russian Cossack brigades. The station chief was usually an elder who worked alongside the local white-beards. Garmeh was the largest trucking depot in the region — most families owned several trucks or rented them. Many were hunters, armed with various Brno rifles and hunting weapons. Taghi the Tabrizi hunter ran the famous Garmeh general store, with his old military jeep — the stop for a cold *Canada Dry* before heading on to Jajarm.

### Jajarm and Garmeh: Knife and Cheese, Friend and Enemy

Jajarm was livelier than even Sangsar. Every conceivable government office and organization — if not run by a Jajarmi, had several Jajarmis embedded in its government-within-a-government. Everyone was related, distantly or closely. One phone call to a family connection could accomplish the impossible.

Legend has it that Jajarm was founded several thousand years ago. A clay fortress in the city center holds coins minted in Jajarm dating back 5,000 years. The founding legend: the people, fed up with the heat of the capital, complained to the government. The officials said, "*If Jajarm is cold, go to Garmeh — it's warm!*" And so Garmeh was founded on the mountain slopes. When the people complained again about Garmeh's heat, the officials said, "*Go* Ivar*!*" — meaning "over there." And Ivar was founded. When the last complaint came, the officials lost patience: "*Go to* Darq*!*" — a pun on "*Go to hell!*" (*Berid be darak*). And so Darq was born.

## Memories

### Dariush and the Calligraphy Wall

I was a child. At that time, Dariush was the manager of the Epicure restaurant in Tehran. He had exquisite handwriting — he could write calligraphy with both hands and produced works that rivaled the masters.

Once, my father took me to visit Dariush. He told this story about the Kargozar: The courtyard walls had just been freshly plastered and whitewashed. Young Dariush, ever the devil and calligraphy-obsessed, took a piece of charcoal and wrote something on the pristine wall. Suddenly, the Kargozar appeared. Dariush said: *"He twisted my ear so hard I lifted off the ground — and it feels like it still hurts to this day!"*

After the scolding was done, the Kargozar looked at what had been written on the wall and said: *"You have beautiful handwriting!"* Then he gave him a substantial coin as a reward. That was his character — discipline first, then recognition. Encouragement and punishment, each in its proper place.

![The Calligraphy Wall](images/Ali-Yar-Fakhran-Calligraphy-Wall.webp)
*AI illustration: Young Dariush writing calligraphy on the freshly whitewashed courtyard wall*

### The Dinner Protocol

My father used to tell this story: When the dinner cloth was spread, the Kargozar would take his seat at the head of the table. Then he would go through the children one by one: *"Whose child are you? Who is your father? Who is your grandfather? Why are you sitting there? Go to the door! Or move two places down! Or come up higher!"*

Until he had arranged everyone according to the rank and title of their forefathers, nobody dared touch the food. Imagine — if he treated his own grandchildren and the children at his own table this way, how rigorous he must have been in official matters.

My father inherited the Kargozar's discipline — but with an extra dose of fussiness. When we built a building, he would collect my cigarette butts from the foundation pit — half an hour before they were to pour concrete over it — and announce: *"There isn't a single cigarette butt in this building!"* My Afghan workers and I would laugh, knowing we'd been burying butts in the mortar and plaster all day. A few had even poked through the whitewash. We dug them out, patched the wall. Dad came, asked why the wall had stains. And the workers, like sheep, said: *"Yes, yes, Mr. Bamdad is right."*

## The Author's Reflection

Unfortunately, my memories of this great ancestor amount to a few names and stories: the tale of the horseback-riding daughter, Dariush's calligraphy, and the dinner table protocol.

My father — *Abui Geram* — was no thief, no cheat, never bullied the weak, never coveted another man's honor. But he was affectionate with all the women and girls in the family — aunts, cousins, in-laws, religious or secular — with kisses and warmth that made the ladies comfortable and the men shake their heads. Ahmad would say: *"Uncle hasn't been to Mecca yet and he's squeezing and rubbing our wives. Now that he's become a Hajji, he's even worse!"* And my father, laughing across the room, would shout: *"Ahmadjan, you men don't take care of your wives — flowers need water and attention! What's it to do with me?"*

I inherited a sharp, biting tongue for high-stakes work — whether as a private consultant for factory installations, CNC machine troubleshooting, and network architecture in Iran, or at **Morgan Stanley**, **Verizon**, **Apple**, and **General Electric** in America. I once bought a book titled *"Operational Amplifiers: Grab and Pull!"* — their Farsi translation of push-pull (*begir-o-bekesh*, بگیر و بکش). The catch: without Arabic diacritical marks, *bekesh* (pull) reads identically to *bokosh* (kill) — so the title looked like *"Grab and Kill!"* to every reader. And *"seesaw"* (*alâkolangí*) for flip-flop. I didn't know whether to laugh or cry.

My language was always good — and long! When I was leaving for America, the consul said: *"Your language is good too."* I said: *"I know it's long! But I think my accent is horrific — I learned English by reading, not listening."*

As Bahman Joon would honestly say: *"Mammadjan, Bamdad's tongue and his way with words are just like the Kargozar's — God rest his soul."*

## Bagher Khan, Hassan Khan, Hussain Khan, and Karim Khan

![The Kargozar's Children](images/Kargozar-Children.webp)
*AI illustration: The Kargozar's children — Bagher Khan, Hassan Khan, Hussain Khan, and Karim Khan*

### Dr. Karim Khan Fakhran — The Village Doctor

**Karim Khan** was a son of the Kargozar. He had a medical practice in Jajarm and was a physician. He was extraordinarily sociable and eloquent. My father's heart beat warmly for Karim Khan and his family.

**Zeenat Joon** (زینت‌خانم) was Karim Khan's wife. Since they were all Khans and sons of Khans, most of us addressed them as "Khan," but the patients — either out of stubbornness or indifference — would simply say "Mr. Doctor."

> **Translation Note:** *Zeenat Joon* is Karim Khan's wife — not "my wife" as machine translations frequently render. The author is the great-grandson of the Kargozar, narrating stories about his father's cousin's family.

Zeenat Joon's cooking was legendary. In her youth, she was a horsewoman and hunter, with impeccable pedigree.

### Firuz

**Firuz** was their eldest son — my friend and *fabrik* companion (childhood friend; literally "factory-made," meaning "original"). He was the same age as my cousins. Mischief aside: one night he and Ahmad went to someone's garden to steal tomatoes. They even brought a salt shaker for the red and green tomatoes and cucumbers! But these sons of Khans — for all their trying — couldn't climb the gate or jump into the garden. Ahmad snapped, chained his left hand to the garden door and yanked it open. The next day, when the mess was discovered, the owner dragged them to their father's garden. *Theft!* What a scandal that became.

Firuz also had an unsuccessful marriage — someone swept him off his feet in an emotional moment. Karim Khan and Zeenat Joon rejected the match. Mojgan was his patient rock, and he would pour his heart out to my father: *"Mammadjan, Firuz... al-Firuz... bal-Faqruz!"* (a play on words — from "Firuz" meaning triumph to "Faqruz" meaning poverty/ruin).

Firuz moved to Mashhad. On the Mashhad-Quchan road, during one of his trips, he had an accident and died young — like my father's older brother, who was thrown by a horse and killed, causing my father to drop out of school for two years.

### The Obsessive Cleanliness

Ali's obsessive cleanliness was hereditary. When he came home from work at night, he would strip off his clothes in the yard, lay out newspapers by the heater, and sleep naked on them until morning — only entering the house after heating the water, bathing, and putting on clean clothes.

Karim Khan's obsession was even worse — wash, scrub, rinse, soap! He once told us about going to Ivar or Darq to deliver a baby. Afterward, they gave him soap to wash his hands, and he was stuck wondering where to put the dirty soap. He asked: *"Where should I put the soap?"* The other person wouldn't put it down or pick it up. Finally: *"Avi ku!"* — meaning, *"You cow! Just put it on another stone!"* Karim Khan told it with his thick accent, and we would collapse laughing.

Firuz, Mojgan, and Zeenat were easygoing and cheerful. Ali, Karim Khan, and Sosan were the opposite — like Auntie, who scrubbed and scrubbed until she gave herself a hundred different skin diseases.

### Chess, Backgammon, and Family Evenings

## The Grandmother's Curse

![Grandmother's Curse](images/Grandmother-Curse.webp)
*AI illustration: A family-memory rendering of the grandmother's sharp curse — “May the desert wolf take you! What blind, bald, wretched man wants you?!”*

Their sister — my father's grandmother — used to say to my mother: *"May the desert wolf take you! What blind, bald, wretched man wants you?!"*

I take after my grandmothers, each one more sharp-tongued than the last. I never met my father's grandmother, but my mother would say: *"Bamdad is a loudmouth, to put it eloquently!"* Or: *"He has a hundred faults and one virtue — he doesn't lie."* Or: *"His shit-stirring spoon is always at his belt."* I'd say: *"Mother, that's TWO virtues now!"* She'd say: *"Vakhi vakhai, my boy!"*

My mother was a Shahrudi — a proverbial gold mine — and I was the parrot of her proverbs, feeding them right back to the family. I knew every last relative, friend, and acquaintance. I have no patience for nonsense. When I was being *very* polite, I might wedge a chestnut into someone's bowl only two or three times. Everyone kept score; whoever was absent got roasted and became the family joke of the year.

My father used to say: *"My poor mother didn't know! The blind, bald, wretched desert wolf — it was her own Mammadek! A spoiled mama's boy after two sisters!"* He was all still waters, deep currents. And I'm the fool — his only son — but I burned his complacency. He'd imitate the Kargozar for me, not realizing he himself was the most Fakhran of them all.

As Bahman Joon would honestly say: *"Mammadjan, Bamdad's tongue and literature are just like the Kargozar's — God rest his soul."*
