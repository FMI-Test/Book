## Translate and Decipher Titles & Name

### Mirza
In 20th-century Iran, the title **Mirza** ([میرزا](https://www.google.com/search?q=https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%25D9%2585%25D9%2582%25D8%25B5%25D9%2588%25D8%25AF%23Persian)) underwent a major transformation, shifting from a mark of high nobility to a general indicator of education before eventually fading into history.

### 1\. The Rule of Placement

The meaning of "Mirza" depended entirely on where it was placed in relation to a person's name. This distinction remained crucial in the early 1900s:

  * **Before the Name (e.g., Mirza Hassan):** This designated a **literate man**, a clerk, or a secretary. In an era where literacy was a specialized skill, being a "Mirza" meant you were part of the professional, educated class.
  * **After the Name (e.g., Abbas Mirza):** This indicated **royal or princely status**. It was reserved for the sons of the Shah or high-ranking members of the Qajar dynasty.

### 2\. Early 20th Century: The Professional Class

During the first three decades of the century, "Mirza" was the standard title for:

  * **Accountants and Scribes:** Known as *Mirza-ye Benevise*, these men handled the financial and administrative records for the government or wealthy merchants.
  * **Government Bureaucrats:** As Iran began building a modern state apparatus, "Mirza" became synonymous with the civil servants working in the newly formed ministries.

### 3\. The 1935 Abolition of Titles

The turning point for the term came in **1935** under [Reza Shah Pahlavi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reza_Shah). As part of a sweeping modernization and "Westernization" program:

  * He officially **abolished all aristocratic and honorary titles**, including *Khan*, *Agha* (in its old sense), and *Mirza*.
  * The goal was to create a sense of national equality. The title "Agha" (placed before the last name) was adopted as the universal, egalitarian address for all Iranian men, similar to "Mr."

### 4\. Late 20th Century: Legacy and Surnames

By the latter half of the 20th century, "Mirza" had almost entirely lost its function as a living title:

  * **Surnames:** It survived primarily as a prefix or suffix in family names, such as *Mirzakhani*, *Mirzazadeh*, or *Amir-Mirza*.
  * **Nostalgia:** If used in conversation, it was often a term of endearment or respect for an elderly, dignified man who still practiced traditional calligraphy or possessed "old-world" manners.

### Agha
In the 20th century, the title **Mirza** ([میرزا](https://www.google.com/search?q=https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%25D9%2585%25D9%2582%25D8%25B5%25D9%2588%25D8%25AF%23Persian)) underwent a significant transition. While it carried heavy weight in the 19th-century Qajar era, it gradually faded or changed meaning as Iran modernized under the Pahlavi dynasty.

### 1\. The "Rule of Placement"

The meaning of Mirza depended entirely on whether it came **before** or **after** a person's name:

  * **Before the Name (e.g., Mirza Reza):** This identified the person as a **literate man**, a scribe, or a government official. In a time when literacy was low, being a "Mirza" meant you were educated and likely handled correspondence or accounting.
  * **After the Name (e.g., Abbas Mirza):** This indicated **royal blood**. It was reserved for princes or descendants of the Shah.

### 2\. Early 20th Century: The Educated Class

In the first few decades of the 1900s, "Mirza" was still widely used for:

  * **Civil Servants:** Bureaucrats working in the growing administrative departments of the state.
  * **Accountants (Mirza-ye Benevise):** Specifically those who kept financial records for merchants or the government.
  * **Religious Scholars:** Occasionally used for junior clerics who were also literate in secular administration.

### 3\. The 1935 Title Reform

A major shift occurred in **1935** under [Reza Shah Pahlavi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reza_Shah). As part of his modernization efforts, he issued a decree **abolishing aristocratic titles**.

  * Titles like *Khan*, *Agha* (in its eunuch sense), and *Mirza* were officially removed from government use to promote social equality.
  * The Western-style "Agha" (placed before the surname) became the standardized way to address all men, regardless of their literacy or lineage.

### 4\. Late 20th Century: Legacy and Surnames

By the mid-to-late 20th century, "Mirza" largely disappeared as an active title and instead:

  * **Became a Surname:** Many families adopted it as part of their last name (e.g., *Mirzakhani* or *Mirzazadeh*).
  * **Became Archaic:** If used at all, it was often used affectionately for an elderly, dignified man who represented the "old world" of manners and calligraphy.

## Khan
In the 19th century, the term **Khan** ([خان](https://dsal.uchicago.edu/cgi-bin/app/hayyim_query.py?qs=%D8%AE%D8%A7%D9%86&matchtype=default)) served as a multifaceted title in Persia (Iran), primarily denoting social status, political authority, or simple politeness.

### 1. A Title of Nobility and Governance
During the **Qajar era** (1789–1925), "Khan" was an extremely common title used for **noblemen, courtiers, and tribal leaders**. 
* **Tribal Authority:** It was the standard title for the heads of powerful nomadic tribes (like the Bakhtiari or Qashqai). These Khans held significant local power, often acting as autonomous rulers over their territories.
* **Bureaucratic Rank:** Within the central government, it was a title bestowed by the Shah upon high-ranking officials and military commanders.

### 2. Evolution Toward Politeness
By the 19th century, the term began to shift from a strictly hereditary or granted noble title toward a **general honorific**. 
* It was attached after a man's name (e.g., *Daryoosh Khan*) to mark **politeness or social distance**. 
* While [**Aqa**](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D8%A2%D9%82%D8%A7#Persian) (Mister) was also used, **Khan** carried a slightly more formal or prestigious weight in 19th-century social hierarchies compared to its more informal use today.

### 3. Etymological Roots
The word has two distinct origins in Persian history:
* **Turko-Mongol Origin:** Borrowed from [Old Turkic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_Turkic) (*qaɣan*), referring to a "ruler" or "king." This is the source of the political title.
* **Middle Persian Origin:** Derived from [Middle Persian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Neil_MacKenzie) (*xān*), meaning "house" or "inn," which led to the word [**Caravanserai**](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/caravanserai#English) (a roadside inn for travelers).

---

In summary, a "Khan" in 19th-century Persia was typically a man of influence—either a tribal chief, a government official, or a member of the landed gentry—though it was increasingly used as a respectful way to address any man of standing.

## Mumtahen-ul-Mulk
In the context of 20th-century Iran, **Mumtahen-ul-Mulk** (Persian: ممتحن‌الملک) was a high-ranking aristocratic title that roughly translates to **"The Examined of the Kingdom"** or **"The Proven of the Realm."** It is most famously associated with **Mirza Mehdi Khan Shaqaqi**, a prominent 19th and early 20th-century figure who was a pioneer of modern architecture and diplomacy in Iran.

### The Meaning and Origin
The title is composed of three Arabic-derived Persian words:
* **Mumtahen (ممتحن):** Meaning "one who has been tested," "examined," or "proven."
* **Al (ال):** The definite article "the."
* **Mulk (ملک):** Meaning "kingdom," "country," or "realm."

In the Qajar era (up until 1925), titles ending in *-ul-Mulk* were granted by the Shah to elite civil servants, diplomats, and intellectuals to denote their service to the state. "Mumtahen-ul-Mulk" specifically implied that the holder was a person of proven merit and expertise who had been "tested" by the state through rigorous education or service.

---

### Mirza Mehdi Khan Shaqaqi (Mumtahen-ul-Mulk)
The title is almost inseparable from the man who held it, Mirza Mehdi Khan (1844–1920). He was a transformative figure in 20th-century Iranian history for several reasons:

* **Pioneer Architect:** He was the first Iranian to be formally trained in academic architecture in Europe (Paris). He designed iconic buildings in Tehran, including the **Sepahsalar Mosque and Madrasa** and the original **Majlis (Parliament) Building**.
* **Diplomat:** He served in various diplomatic roles, representing Iran in Europe and eventually serving in the Ministry of Foreign Affairs.
* **The "Examined" Title:** He allegedly received the title because of his extensive and successful examinations in France, which were unprecedented for an Iranian student at the time.
* **Memoirs:** His autobiography, *Khaterat-e Mumtahen-ul-Mulk*, is considered one of the most valuable primary sources for understanding the social and political transition from the Qajar period to the early 20th century.

---

### Cultural Significance
By the early 20th century, such titles were becoming symbols of the "Old Guard." In 1935, under Reza Shah Pahlavi, these elaborate aristocratic titles were officially abolished in favor of modern, fixed surnames. Many families, including descendants of Mirza Mehdi Khan, eventually adopted their titles or variations of them (like **Shaqaqi** or **Momtahen**) as their permanent family names.

Building on the history of that title, it is fascinating how it transitioned from a formal bureaucratic designation to a prestigious family legacy in 20th-century Iran.

* **Summary:** In the Qajar era, it was an aristocratic title given to individuals who had demonstrated exceptional expertise—often through rigorous Western education or high-level diplomatic service—signifying they were "tested" and "approved" by the state.

During the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the Iranian government used these titles to categorize the elite. A title ending in *-ul-Mulk* was among the highest honors for civil servants.

### The Legacy of Mirza Mehdi Khan
As we discussed, the title is most famously tied to **Mirza Mehdi Khan Shaqaqi**. His life perfectly illustrates why the title was granted:
* **Academic "Testing":** He was the first Iranian to earn an architectural degree in Paris. His "testing" was literal—he had to prove his skills in a foreign system before bringing that knowledge back to modernize Iran.
* **Diplomatic Service:** He served as a bridge between Iran and Europe, specifically in the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, embodying the "Proven" nature of the title through his loyalty and skill.

The year 1935 was a turning point. **Reza Shah Pahlavi** abolished these titles to modernize the country and eliminate the old feudal class system. 
* Holders of the title had to choose permanent surnames.
* Many chose **Momtahen**, **Shaqaqi**, or **Momtahen-ol-Molk** as their official last name to preserve their family's history.

In her youth, Zinatakhanum was a horseman and hunter, with a pedigree. When Bahmanjun came, Baba and I would either go with Firuz to the Bahmanjun garden and farm, or hunt, hunt meat, and have fun. It was a sweet time. Everyone was either related or knew us. Firuz and Ali were the grandchildren of the broker and the children of the village doctor. My father was the mayor and the head of the bank, and I had a bunch of cousins, each one more evil and famous than the other. The head of the Jajarm court — who is now the head of the Revolutionary Court — was a friend of Baba’s Fabrik.

Their sister, who was my father's grandmother, used to say to my mother, "May the desert wolf take you! What blind, bald, wretched man wants to get you?!" I've been to my grandmothers, each one more eloquent than the other! I didn't meet my father's grandmother, but my mother would say, "He's a big talker, literally!" Or she would say, "He's got a hundred bad things in the morning, one good thing—he doesn't lie." Or, "His spoon for eating is always his waist." I would say, "Mother, he's got two!" He would say, "Wakhi, wakhai, uncle!" Shahrudi was a proverbial mine, and I was his proverbial parrot, and I would pass them on to them. I knew all of his family and friends. I don't have the patience to be lazy. If I was very careful, I would stick a plantain in the other person's bowl two or three times. They had a plan; whoever was absent would be slapped with a soap opera and become the family joke of the year.

My father used to say: "My poor mother didn't know! The blind, bald, miserable wolf of the desert, Mamedek is his own son — a spoiled, spoiled boy after two sisters!" It was water under the straw for him. I am poor; my only son, but I put the itch in his mourning. He would pretend to be a servant to me, he didn't know that he himself was a proud man! Bahmanjon would honestly say: "Mamedjon, in the morning his language and literature are like a servant, may God have mercy on him." ## Karim Khan Fakhran

Karim Khan was the son of a clerk. He had a practice in Jajarm and was a doctor. I have interesting memories of him; he was very sociable and eloquent. My father also had a heart for Karim Khan and his family. My wife, Zeenat Khan, was Karim Khan's wife. Since they were all Khans and Khans' sons, most of us addressed them as "Khan," but the patients, either out of stubbornness or indifference, would say "Mr. Doctor."

My wife's cooking was amazing. Firuz was their eldest son and my friend and comrade in the factory—the same age as my cousins. Evil and evil aside; one night they had gone to a certain garden with Ahmad to steal some tomatoes. They had also taken a salt shaker to put in the red and green tomatoes and cucumbers! But no matter how hard they tried, these sons of the family could not climb over the door or jump into the garden. Ahmad also tried, chained his left hand to the garden door and pulled it with a jerk until the door opened. The next day, when he was sick, they took him to his father's garden—theft! What a subject he had become.

Let alone that the child Firuz had an unsuccessful marriage; he was kidnapped and I think they got married in an emotional or moral situation. Karim Khan and Zeenat Khanum rejected him. Mojgan was his patient rock and he was most upset with my father. “Mammadjan, Firuz... Al-Firouz... Bal-Faqrouz!” Firuz went to Mashhad and on the Mashhad-Quchan road, during one of these trips, he had an accident and died young — like my father’s older brother who was run over by a horse and died, and my father dropped out of school for two years.

Later, my father met Colonel Dadras — the head of the Mashhad and Bojnourd Gendarmerie Regiments during the revolution — and Dr. Asterabadi, a pharmacist. Dr. Asterabadi knew my doctor’s uncle, who was a pharmacist. Uncle Ahmad had taken Aunt Farzaneh. My father also became a benefactor and introduced Foroughjoon to my uncle, in the villas of the former Keshavarzi Bank — the same Islamic Banking Sciences Faculty of Keshavarzi Bank that my father had become the head of after the revolution. Mr. Kalani — his uncle — had also become the head of the Bojnourd Civil Registry.

When we went to Bojnourd, I just found out that my grandfather had the title of "Prophet" because he was a man of prayer and fasting, and neither sweat nor women—and I didn't know either! For a while he smoked cigarettes; with my force, he even beat a courier with me. Until he passed away, he went to Mecca and said, "I have become a pilgrim and that's it, Mammadjan." My grandfather died.

Ali was also obsessed with Taghari! When he came home from work at night, he would take off his clothes in the yard, pick up a few newspapers, throw them by the stove, and sleep naked on the newspapers until morning—until they turned on the water heater, went to the bathroom, put on clean clothes, and came in.

Karim Khan was also terribly obsessed—wash, rub, soap! He once told me that he had gone to Iver or Darq to give birth. Then when he came to wash his hands, they gave him soap; he was left wondering where to put the soap. He asked, “Where should I put the soap?” The other person wouldn’t put it down or pick it up. He said, “Avi ku”—meaning, “Cow! Put your feet on another stone!” Karim Khan told me in an accent, and we would burst out laughing.

My chess and board games got better there, because everyone was playing chess and backgammon. When my father was embarrassed, I would sing a song for Karim Khan. He would say, “Mammadjan, this morning he is the proudest of all! Like his father, no one can compete with him!” Firuz would come and say, “Let me rap on this little boy.” I would say, “Mammadjan, is that allowed?” He would say, “Firuzjon, are you calling me a little boy, are you going to apologize to your father later?” He would say, “You are still a fool!” — I would give him a piece of bread with two hands, and take it with three hands. Mojgan would say, “Come on, father, it’s my job!” Mojgan was also a cousin, couldn’t she fight him? She would take the dice, cast a spell on Hanbal. Mojgan and I would play so much that Dad would get bored and say, "Leave me alone, my cousin, what are you doing? Dinner is ready!"

Karim Khan used to tell stories: A man goes to him and says, "Doctor, my stomach hurts." He says, "Okay, tell me what you did? What did you eat?" He says, "I went to see my cousin, who is a girl..." The cousin says, "Where did you go, Dad?" Karim Khan says, "Let him go, tell him what you think!" He says, "Okay, Doctor, you won't!" In short, when Hassan Kurd finishes his story, Karim Khan angrily says, "You bastard! What does this have to do with your stomach ache? I'll tell you what you ate, you got hit in the stomach, what the hell?" And he says, "What do I know? Are you a doctor or a cow?!"

Now he had eaten his greed and was telling us stories and we were laughing. Then Baba and Karim Khan and Ali and Mojgan and Zeinat Khanum and Mom and Maryam and Ali and I would start telling jokes. My jokes never ended. I would only wear my eyebrows; I wouldn't tell the ones with the top in front of Mojgan and Zeinat Khanum. Baba would whisper everything in their ears and Mojgan would faint and become a thread. In short, it was a sweet time.

Colonel Hassan Khan and Colonel Hussein Khan were also Karim Khan's brothers. They prayed but without ablution. I said, "Sir Colonel, don't you perform ablution?" He said, "Bamdadjan, ablution is for cleanliness, I am always clean too." I once made a mistake - in front of Karim Khan, I said to Colonel Hassan Khan, "You mean you have never farted in your life, Colonel?" He said a firm "no."
